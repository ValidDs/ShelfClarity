# ShelfClarity Validation Path

Status: Draft validation plan  
Owner: Chris  
Prepared by: Professor  
Date: 2026-05-01

## 1. Validation Goal

ShelfClarity does not need validation to prove that beauty routine confusion exists. That pain is obvious. It needs validation to prove that users will trust and pay a new product to help organize beauty products and routines.

The core validation question:

**Will beauty shoppers upload their products, trust a cautious audit, and pay $49 or more for a practical keep, pause, replace, and next-buy plan?**

If the answer is yes, ShelfClarity is worth serious product development.

## 2. What Must Be Proven

### Must prove

1. Users understand the promise quickly.
2. Users are willing to upload shelf/product photos.
3. Users accept the safety-first language and still find the product valuable.
4. Users will pay for a full audit, not only join a waitlist.
5. The output feels specific, credible, and useful.
6. Users feel the product reduced confusion or stopped a bad purchase.
7. Users would recommend it to someone with too many products.

### Does not need to prove yet

1. Full automation.
2. Perfect product recognition.
3. Subscription retention.
4. Mobile app retention.
5. Dermatologist-grade authority.
6. Marketplace revenue.
7. Full ingredient database coverage.

## 3. Validation Hypotheses

### Hypothesis 1: The hook is clear

Users will understand “Your beauty shelf, audited” without much explanation.

Pass signal:

- 40 percent or more of landing page visitors scroll past hero or click a CTA.
- 20 percent or more start the quiz/upload flow.

Fail signal:

- Users ask “what does this do?” after reading the hero.
- Users interpret it as medical skincare advice or a shopping app only.

### Hypothesis 2: Users will upload products

Users with enough pain will upload shelf photos or product photos.

Pass signal:

- 30 percent or more of quiz starters upload at least one image.
- Average uploaded products per serious user is 8 or more.

Fail signal:

- Users abandon at upload because it feels too private, too much work, or too risky.

### Hypothesis 3: Users will pay for the audit

Users will pay because the audit helps clarify products they already bought.

Pass signal:

- 10 to 25 percent of free-preview users buy the full report.
- At least 10 paid audits from non-family/non-close-friend users.
- Refund requests below 8 percent.

Fail signal:

- Users love the concept but refuse to pay.
- Users only want free AI chat.

### Hypothesis 4: Safety language does not kill desire

The product can be cautious without becoming useless.

Pass signal:

- Users say the caution language makes them trust the product more.
- Users still rate output as practical and actionable.

Fail signal:

- Users say recommendations feel too vague to justify paying.

## 4. Validation MVP

Do not build a full app first.

Build a concierge MVP:

1. Landing page.
2. Quiz.
3. Upload form.
4. Free preview output.
5. Paid full audit.
6. Manual/human QA behind the scenes.
7. Delivered web/PDF report.

### Free preview

The free preview should show just enough value to prove specificity.

Example preview:

- “We found 17 products.”
- “You appear to have 4 treatment products, 3 moisturizers, and no confirmed daily SPF.”
- “Two products may overlap in exfoliating role.”
- “Unlock the full keep, pause, replace, and routine plan.”

### Paid audit

The paid report includes:

- Product inventory.
- Keep/pause/replace/use carefully labels.
- Confidence levels.
- Why each product was flagged.
- AM routine.
- PM routine.
- Weekly rhythm.
- Missing essentials.
- Next best purchase category.
- Budget cleanup notes.
- Safety disclaimers.

## 5. Pricing Test

Test pricing in this order:

1. $49 Full Shelf Audit.
2. $89 Premium Shelf Audit.
3. Optional $29 limited audit only if conversion is too low.

Do not start too cheap. Cheap can make the product feel less credible and can attract low-intent users.

Recommended beta pricing:

- Free preview: $0.
- Full Shelf Audit: $49.
- Premium Shelf Audit: $89.

## 6. Acquisition Tests

### Channel 1: TikTok/Reels style content

Concepts:

- “Your shelf is not a routine.”
- “You probably do not need five serums.”
- “What I would pause from this shelf.”
- “Stop buying before you audit.”

Goal:

- Drive visitors to landing page.

### Channel 2: Beauty communities

Use carefully. Do not spam.

Possible places:

- Reddit skincare communities.
- Haircare communities.
- Beauty Discords.
- Facebook groups.

Goal:

- Get qualitative feedback and first paid beta users.

### Channel 3: Micro-influencer audit

Offer 5 to 10 free audits to micro creators in exchange for honest feedback/content.

Goal:

- See if the audit format is shareable.

## 7. Research Questions

Ask beta users:

1. What made you interested enough to upload your products?
2. What worried you before uploading?
3. Which part of the report felt most useful?
4. Which recommendation did you not trust?
5. Would you pay $49 for this again or recommend it?
6. Did this stop you from buying anything?
7. Did this make your routine simpler?
8. Did the safety language make it more or less credible?

## 8. Success Criteria

### Green light

Move forward if:

- 10 or more users pay for audits.
- 70 percent or more rate report useful.
- 50 percent or more say it changed what they will use or buy.
- At least 30 percent say they would recommend it.
- Users describe the output as specific, not generic AI.

### Yellow light

Continue testing if:

- Users upload products but payment conversion is weak.
- Users like the idea but want more proof/sample output.
- Users trust the report but find price high.

### Red light

Pause or rethink if:

- Users do not upload products.
- Users do not trust the output.
- Users only want free advice.
- Safety language makes the report feel toothless.
- Reports require too much manual work to be profitable.

## 9. Validation Timeline

### Week 1

- Launch visual landing page.
- Create example audit report.
- Set up waitlist/payment interest form.
- Recruit 10 to 20 beta users manually.

### Week 2

- Run first 5 concierge audits.
- Interview users.
- Refine report format.
- Test $49 price.

### Week 3

- Run 10 to 20 more audits.
- Test free preview to paid conversion.
- Identify common product categories and audit rules.

### Week 4

- Decide whether to build automated MVP.

## 10. Decision Rule

ShelfClarity should become the primary product only if people pay for the audit before full automation exists.

If people will not pay for a human-reviewed audit, they probably will not pay for the app.
